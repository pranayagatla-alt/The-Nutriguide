EYE SIGHT

Sight (also called eyesight or vision) is one of the senses. Having sight means to be able to see. Seeing gives animals knowledge of the world. Some simple animals can only tell light from dark, but with vertebrates, the visual system is able to form images. The ability to interpret visible light information reaching the eyes is called visual perception. Sight is the resulting perception. The components that are necessary for vision are known as the visual system. The major problem in visual perception is that what people see is not simply a translation of an image on the retina. The key, which took centuries to be appreciated, is that the brain works on the data from the eyes, and marries it with memories and guesses, all at lightning speed. The result is an experience of the world which looks to each person as if it were simple reality. However, although based on reality, it is actually a mental construct, built by the brain. Eyesight issues often stem from refractive errors (myopia, hyperopia, astigmatism) caused by abnormal eye shape or lens flexibility, and structural eye diseases like cataracts, glaucoma, or macular degeneration. Major causes of vision loss include uncorrected refractive errors (42%), cataracts (33%), and retinal diseases, which are managed via lenses, surgery, or medication.Key Causes of Eyesight Impairment Refractive Errors (Most Common):Myopia (Nearsightedness): Caused by the eye growing too long, creating a focus point in front of the retina. Driven by genetics, indoor time, and close-up work.Hyperopia (Farsightedness): Occurs when the eye is too short, focusing images behind the retina, often affecting children.Presbyopia: Age-related loss of lens flexibility, making close-up focus difficult, affecting most people over 35-55.Astigmatism: Blurred vision caused by an irregularly shaped cornea or lens.Major Eye Diseases:Cataracts: Clouding of the lens, frequently leading to blindness, especially in older adults.Glaucoma: A group of diseases that damage the eye's optic nerve, often causing peripheral vision loss (tunnel vision).Macular Degeneration: Age-related malfunction of the macula, causing loss of central vision.Retinopathy: Damage to the retina, including diabetic retinopathy (diabetes complications) and retinopathy of prematurity.Other Causes:Vitamin A Deficiency: Causes night blindness due to lack of rhodopsin regeneration.Infections/Injuries: Such as trachoma or trauma causing corneal opacity.Neurological Factors: Injuries to the visual cortex causing scotoma or hemianopia.
